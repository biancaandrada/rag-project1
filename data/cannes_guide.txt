Cannes is globally known for the Cannes Film Festival, but it is also a coastal city with a mix of luxury tourism and local charm. It is smaller and more relaxed than Nice, making it suitable for shorter visits.

The Boulevard de la Croisette is the main promenade, lined with luxury hotels, beaches, and designer shops. It runs along the coastline and is central to the city’s identity.

Le Suquet is the old town of Cannes, located on a hill. It offers narrow streets, local restaurants, and panoramic views of the port. Compared to the modern parts of Cannes, Le Suquet provides a more authentic atmosphere.

The Palais des Festivals is where the famous film festival takes place. Visitors can see the red carpet area and the handprints of celebrities.

Cannes also serves as a gateway to the Lérins Islands, located just offshore. Île Sainte-Marguerite is the most popular, known for its natural environment and historical fort.

Transportation is similar to Nice. The TER train connects Cannes with Nice and Monaco. Within the city, most areas are walkable.

Cannes is generally more expensive than average French cities but less extreme than Monaco. It is best experienced as a combination of sightseeing, beach relaxation, and short excursions.

The French Riviera, particularly Nice, offers a strong food culture influenced by both French and Italian traditions. Local cuisine emphasizes fresh ingredients, olive oil, vegetables, seafood, and simple preparation techniques.

One of the most iconic local foods is socca, a chickpea-based flatbread cooked in a wood-fired oven. It is commonly found in traditional eateries rather than modern restaurants. Pissaladière, another regional specialty, is a savory tart made with caramelized onions, anchovies, and olives.

Bakeries (boulangeries) are essential to daily life in France. A high-quality boulangerie produces fresh bread multiple times a day. The baguette tradition is particularly important, with strict regulations defining its ingredients and preparation.

In Nice, top boulangeries include small artisan shops rather than chains. The best indicators of quality are long queues, limited product variety, and visible baking activity.

Patisseries focus on desserts and pastries. Croissants, pain au chocolat, and tarts are standard, but high-end patisseries also offer complex creations such as mille-feuille and éclairs with modern variations.

For an authentic experience, it is recommended to avoid heavily touristic locations and instead choose places frequented by locals. Early morning visits usually provide the freshest products.

Food culture in this region is not just about eating but also about routine, quality, and tradition. Understanding where locals eat is key to finding the best experiences.
